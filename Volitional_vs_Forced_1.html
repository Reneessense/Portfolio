Volitional vs. Forced

A Field Study of Just-in-Time Interventions

Abstract

This research investigates the effectiveness of different types of digital attention recovery interventions—specifically comparing "volitional" (user-initiated) and "forced" (system-initiated) breaks. Through a field study, we deploy and test a system designed to prompt digital workers to step away from their screens. We analyze the impact of intervention type on perceived autonomy, task performance, and long-term user adherence, providing critical insights for designing psychologically sound just-in-time nudges in ubiquitous computing environments.

Methodology

We conducted a 48-hour field study with participants engaged in typical digital work. The study utilized a custom-built software tool that randomly assigned participants to either the volitional or forced intervention group. Data collection included objective metrics (screen time, task completion rate) and subjective reports (surveys on well-being and perceived control).

Key Findings

  • The volitional intervention group reported significantly higher levels of autonomy and motivation for future break-taking.
  • While forced interventions provided immediate attention recovery, their long-term sustainability was lower due to psychological reactance.
  • We developed a set of design recommendations for implementing autonomy-supportive features in JIT systems.
