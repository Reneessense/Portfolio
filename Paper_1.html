The Categorization of Indigenous Beliefs in Pluralistic Healing Experiences

A study and discussion on the overlapping therapeutic spaces of Primary Religions, Mainstream Religions, and Modern Medicine.

Category: Comparative Mysticism (比较灵修学), Psychology of Religion, Cultural Anthropology | Date: Dec 2022

The study of spiritual experience (灵修体验) reflects a methodological individualistic tendency, compensating for the shortcomings of collective religious studies, and bridging the private and public spheres of religious experience. It is closely linked to the **sacredness (神圣性)** of religion, constituting its most unique aspect that distinguishes it from other ideological systems. By comparing the spiritual methods and experiences of different religions, one can better grasp the fundamental functions, forms, and unique characteristics of individual religions.

Inspiration and Model Construction from Pluralistic Healing Experiences

The author previously reported on the topic: “Witchcraft, Christianity, and Modern Medicine: How is Healing Accomplished?” Inspired by "The Australian Aboriginal Way to Seek Medical Help," the therapeutic space can be divided into: **Marpurn (Indigenous Beliefs), the Christian Church, and the Health Clinic (Modern Medicine)**. This tension (张力) exists similarly in Taiwan during the Japanese colonial period and in minority regions of China.

The author attempts to integrate the tension between Marpurn, Yi Bimo beliefs (彝族毕摩信仰) and other Primary Religions (原初宗教), Christianity and other Mainstream Religions (主流宗教), and Modern Medicine (现代医学) into a relationship model of commonality, complementarity, and opposition among witchcraft, religion, and medicine. The therapeutic spaces of these three do not completely exclude each other, nor can they be fully integrated.

Categorization of Indigenous Beliefs and Model Revision

In the initial model, the distinction between the connotations of witchcraft (巫术) and religion (宗教) was unclear, leading to a dilemma when incorporating Taoism (道教): as a "witchcraft-infused religion (巫化的宗教)," it was difficult to define it as either witchcraft or religion.

Consequently, the author revised the tripartite model of **“Witchcraft-Religion-Medicine”** to **“Indigenous Beliefs (本土信仰)-External Religion (外来宗教)-Modern Medicine”**. This revision emphasizes the relationship between the belief and a specific territory: Indigenous Beliefs struggle to exist outside a certain geographical area, and their adherents are mechanically united by factors like kinship (血缘) and geography (地缘). This necessitates a further examination of the connotation of Indigenous Beliefs, specifically whether to comprehensively include witchcraft, primary religions, and Shamanism (萨满教).

  • Shamanism (萨满教) and Primary Religion: Shamanism is often regarded as the paradigm of animism (万物有灵论). It is associated with hunting societies and societies practicing animal husbandry, fulfilling the vital function of allocating living resources. Healing in Shamanism is based on the body composed of the soul and life force, representing a compensation for human consumption of animal life force, and thus an equal exchange system.
  • The Connotation and Limitations of Shamanism: Shamanism also attempts to create an advantageous position for humans through rituals like imitating the deer and ancestor worship (祖先祭祀), promoting a **“Deliberate Optimism (刻意的乐观主义)”**. However, classifying rituals merely through behavior (e.g., Sky Burial/天葬 ceremony) is ambiguous. Dissolving all primary religions with similar logic into the grand context of “Shamanism” overlooks the characteristics of its hunter-gatherer society origin.

The Logic of Healing: A Dichotomy Criterion for Witchcraft/Religion

When describing the tension of multicultural medical experiences faced by indigenous peoples, the distinction should disregard performative elements (表演性元素) and therapeutic methods, instead relying on the differences in the fundamental logic of the healing plan. This is also the main criterion used by 19th-century anthropologists to distinguish between witchcraft and religion: namely, the **ability to control supernatural powers**.

// Examples of Differences in Healing Logic
- In "The Sorceress (女巫师)": The logic of witchcraft healing is Exorcising/Appeasing Ghosts (驱鬼/安抚鬼魂); The logic of Christian healing is Blessing from God (天主赐福).
- In "The Way to Seek Medical Help (求诊之道)": The logic of Marpurn healing is Driving out Evil Spirits (驱赶恶灵); The logic of the Christian church leader is Prayer and Anointing (祈祷赐油).

The difference lies in whether the practitioner acts as the source of the healing power or as a medium between the patient and the healing power. This broadly aligns with the binary distinction between witchcraft and religion.

Therefore, the **land consciousness (土地意识)** and cultural background associated with primary religions are crucial. The indigenous beliefs of aboriginals can be referred to as “Primary Religion” under a temporally remote understanding. Ultimately, the categorization of **“Primary Religion/Mainstream Religion/Modern Medicine”** is generally valid, and it provides a more vivid description of the dynamic conflict of multiple beliefs than the dichotomy of witchcraft/religion.
